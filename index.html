1. Explain feature selection algorithms and selection criterion. (10)

Answer

Wrapper feature selection tries to find subsets of features, of some fixed size, that will do the trick. However, as anyone who has studied combinations and permutations knows, the number of possible size kkk﻿ subsets of nnn﻿ things, called (nk)\binom{n}{k}(kn​)﻿, grows exponentially. So there’s a nasty opportunity for overfitting by doing this.

There are two aspects to wrappers that you need to consider:

  1. Selecting an algorithm to use to select features and
  1. Deciding on a selection criterion or filter to decide that your set of features is “good.”

Selecting an Algorithm

Let’s first talk about a set of algorithms that fall under the category of stepwise regression, a method for feature selection that involves selecting features according to some selection criterion by either adding or subtracting features to a regression model in a systematic way.

There are three primary methods of stepwise regression: forward selection, backward elimination, and a combined approach (forward and backward).

Forward Selection

In forward selection you start with a regression model with no features, and gradually add one feature at a time according to which feature improves the model the most based on a selection criterion.

This looks like this: build all possible regression models with a single predictor. Pick the best. Now try all possible models that include that best predictor and a second predictor. Pick the best of those. You keep adding one feature at a time, and you stop when your selection criterion no longer improves, but instead gets worse.

Backward Elimination

In backward elimination you start with a regression model that includes all the features, and you gradually remove one feature at a time according to the feature whose removal makes the biggest improvement in the selection criterion. You stop removing features when removing the feature makes the selection criterion get worse.

Combined Approach

Most subset methods are capturing some flavor of minimum-redundancy-maximum-relevance. So, for example, you could have a greedy algorithm that starts with the best feature, takes a few more highly ranked, removes the worst, and so on. This a hybrid approach with a filter method.

Selection Criterion

There are a number of selection criteria you could choose from. As a data scientist you have to select which selection criterion to use. Yes! You need a selection criterion to select the selection criterion.

Part of what we wish to impart to you is that in practice, despite the theoretical properties of these various criteria, the choice you make is somewhat arbitrary.

One way to deal with this is to try different selection criteria and see how robust your choice of model is. Different selection criterion might produce wildly different models, and it’s part of your job to decide what to optimize for and why:

2. Define Feature Extraction. Explain different categories of information. (10)

Answer

This process we just went through of brainstorming a list of features is the process of feature generation or feature extraction.

This process is as much of an art as a science. It’s good to have a domain expert around for this process, but it’s also good to use your imagination.

In today’s technology environment, we’re in a position where we can generate tons of features through logging. Contrast this with other contexts like surveys, for example—you’re lucky if you can get a survey respondent to answer 20 questions, let alone hundreds.

But how many of these features are just noise? In this environment, when you can capture a lot of data, not all of it might be actually useful information.

Keep in mind that ultimately you’re limited in the features you have access to in two ways: whether or not it’s possible to even capture the information, and whether or not it even occurs to you at all to try to capture it. You can think of information as falling into the following buckets:

Relevant and useful, but it’s impossible to capture it.

You should keep in mind that there’s a lot of information that you’re not capturing about users—how much free time do they actually have? What other apps have they downloaded? Are they unemployed? Do they suffer from insomnia? Do they have an addictive personality? Do they have nightmares about dragons?

Some of this information might be more predictive of whether or not they return next month. There’s not much you can do about this, except that it’s possible that some of the data you are able to capture serves as a proxy by being highly correlated with these unobserved pieces of information: e.g., if they play the game every night at 3 a.m., they might suffer from insomnia, or they might work the night shift.

Relevant and useful, possible to log it, and you did.

Thankfully it occurred to you to log it during your brainstorming session. It’s great that you chose to log it, but just because you chose to log it doesn’t mean you know that it’s relevant or useful, so that’s what you’d like your feature selection process to discover.

Relevant and useful, possible to log it, but you didn’t.

It could be that you didn’t think to record whether users uploaded a photo of themselves to their profile, and this action is highly predictive of their likelihood to return. You’re human, so sometimes you’ll end up leaving out really important stuff, but this shows that your own imagination is a constraint in feature selection.

One of the key ways to avoid missing useful features is by doing usability studies, to help you think through the user experience and what aspects of it you’d like to capture.

Not relevant or useful, but you don’t know that and log it.

This is what feature selection is all about—you’ve logged it, but you don’t actually need it and you’d like to be able to know that.

Not relevant or useful, and you either can’t capture it or it didn’t occur to you.

That’s OK! It’s not taking up space, and you don’t need it.

3. Explain Random Forest Classifier. (10)

Answer

Random Forest Algorithm

The Random Forest algorithm is a powerful ensemble learning method that generalizes decision trees with bagging, also known as bootstrap aggregating. Here is a step-by-step description of the algorithm:

  1. Bootstrap Sampling:
    • For each tree, take a bootstrap sample of the data. A bootstrap sample is a sample taken with replacement, meaning the same data point can be selected more than once. Typically, the sample size is 80% of the total dataset size, but this parameter can be adjusted.
  1. Random Feature Selection:
    • For each node in the tree, randomly select a subset of features (e.g., 5 out of 100 total features) to consider for splitting. This introduces additional randomness into the model and helps to de-correlate the trees.
  1. Tree Construction:
    • Use an entropy-information-gain engine to decide which among the selected features to split on at each stage. The decision is based on maximizing the information gain (i.e., the reduction in entropy).
  1. Tree Depth:
    • You can decide beforehand how deep the tree should be or allow the tree to grow fully without pruning. Random forests typically do not prune trees because they can incorporate idiosyncratic noise, which helps in capturing the data’s inherent variability.
  1. Aggregation:
    • Repeat the above steps to build a specified number of trees (e.g., 100 or 500 trees). The final prediction is made by aggregating the predictions of all the trees (majority voting for classification or averaging for regression).

Feature Selection Using Random Forests

Random Forests are also a powerful tool for feature selection due to the following properties:

Importance Scores

Random Forests provide an importance score for each feature, which indicates how useful the feature is in predicting the target variable. This score is based on the decrease in node impurity (e.g., Gini impurity or entropy) brought by the feature.

Embedded Method

Unlike filter methods, which evaluate each feature independently, and wrapper methods, which evaluate subsets of features, Random Forests evaluate features in the context of the model being built. This embedded approach naturally selects features that are most relevant to the prediction task while constructing the trees.

Handling Redundancy

Random Forests handle redundant features well because they are based on multiple trees that consider different random subsets of features. This means that even if some features are highly correlated, the model will not be overly dependent on any single feature.

Model Interpretation

By ranking features based on their importance scores, Random Forests allow for easy identification of the most influential features in the dataset. This can be particularly useful in domains where understanding the contributing factors to a prediction is as important as the prediction itself.

Handling Overfitting in Random Forests

  • Random Forests manage overfitting through the process of bagging (bootstrap aggregating) and the random selection of features.
  • By averaging the predictions of multiple decision trees, Random Forests reduce the variance of the model.
  • Additionally, since each tree is trained on a different random subset of the data and features, the model avoids the overfitting that might occur if a single tree were to learn too many details from the training data.
  • The combination of these techniques makes Random Forests a powerful tool for creating accurate and generalized models.

4. Explain Principal Component Analysis. (10)

Answer

Introduction

  • Principal Component Analysis (PCA) is a statistical technique used for dimensionality reduction, which transforms a large set of variables into a smaller one that still contains most of the information in the large set.
  • PCA is widely used in data science and machine learning to reduce the dimensionality of datasets, making them easier to explore and visualize while retaining essential patterns and relationships.

Objective of PCA

  • The main goal of PCA is to identify the underlying structure in the data by finding the directions (principal components) along which the variation in the data is maximal.
  • This involves transforming the original variables into new variables (principal components) that are uncorrelated, and ordered so that the first few retain most of the variation present in all of the original variables.

Optimization Problem

    • (ui)( u_i )(ui​)﻿ represents the row of (U)( U )(U)﻿ corresponding to the (i)( i )(i)﻿-th user or data point.
    • (vj)( v_j)(vj​)﻿ represents the row of (V)( V )(V)﻿ corresponding to the (j)( j )(j)﻿-th feature or variable.

Interpretation of Principal Components

  • The principal components found by PCA are essentially new variables constructed as linear combinations of the original variables. These components are ordered by the amount of variance they explain in the data, with the first principal component explaining the most variance.

Dimensionality Reduction

  • By choosing a smaller number of principal components (say ddd﻿), PCA reduces the dimensionality of the data while retaining most of the variation. This allows for more efficient data processing and visualization.
  • The number ddd﻿ typically chosen is around 100, but it can vary depending on the context and the amount of computational resources available.

Applications of PCA

  • Data Visualization: PCA is often used to reduce the dimensionality of data to two or three components, making it possible to visualize complex datasets.
  • Noise Reduction: By focusing on the components that capture the most variance, PCA can help filter out noise from the data.
  • Feature Extraction: PCA can be used to identify new, more meaningful features in the data.

8. Briefly explain alternating Least squares methods. (6)

Answer

Introduction

Alternating Least Squares (ALS) is an iterative optimization algorithm used to solve problems where the objective is to find two matrices UUU﻿ and VVV﻿ that approximate a given data matrix XXX﻿.

This technique is commonly applied in matrix factorization problems, such as those encountered in recommendation systems.

Key Concept

The goal of ALS is to minimize the squared error between the actual matrix XXX﻿ and its approximation U⋅VτU \cdot V^\tauU⋅Vτ﻿ by finding the best choices of UUU﻿ and VVV﻿.

ALS simultaneously minimizes the error and the size of the entries in UUU﻿ and VVV﻿, ensuring the solution is regularized to avoid overfitting.

Algorithm Steps

  1. Initialization: Start with a random matrix VVV﻿.
  1. Optimize UUU﻿: While keeping VVV﻿ fixed, optimize UUU﻿.
  1. Optimize VVV﻿: While keeping UUU﻿ fixed, optimize VVV﻿.
  1. Iteration and Convergence:
    1. Continue alternating between optimizing UUU﻿ and VVV﻿ until the changes in the matrices become negligible.
    1. A convergence criterion is set based on a small threshold ϵ\epsilonϵ﻿, and the algorithm stops when the updates to UUU﻿ and VVV﻿ fall below this threshold.

Singular Value Decomposition (SVD)

SVD is a mathematical technique used to decompose a matrix into three component matrices, typically denoted as UUU﻿, Σ\SigmaΣ﻿, and VTV^TVT﻿.

Specifically, for a given matrix AAA﻿, the SVD is expressed as A=UΣVTA = U \Sigma V^TA=UΣVT﻿, where

  • UUU﻿ and VVV﻿ are pairwise orthogonal matrices.
  • Σ\SigmaΣ﻿ is a diagonal matrix containing singular values.

In the context of machine learning and data analysis, SVD helps in reducing the dimensionality of data by approximating the original matrix AAA﻿ with a lower-rank matrix.

This is achieved by retaining only the largest singular values and their corresponding singular vectors.

The reduced matrix captures the most significant features of the data, thereby simplifying the model while preserving essential information.
